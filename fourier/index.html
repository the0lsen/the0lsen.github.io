How does this work?

This tool turns music into spinning circles. Below, we build up the idea one step at a time — no advanced math needed, just a little curiosity.

1 What is a sine wave?

Imagine a dot moving around a circle at a steady pace. Now track only its height over time. That gently rising-and-falling curve is a sine wave — the purest kind of sound, a single steady tone.

The radius of the circle sets the wave's amplitude (how loud it is). How fast the dot spins sets the frequency (how high the pitch is).

2 Frequency is pitch

Spin the circle twice as fast and the wave bunches up — twice as many wiggles in the same time. Your ear hears that as a note exactly one octave higher.

Here the blue circle spins at one speed, and the gold circle spins twice as fast. Double the frequency, double the pitch class.

3 Adding waves makes chords

Play two notes at once and their waves add together, point by point. Where both push up, the result is taller; where they fight, it shrinks.

The gold curve is the sum of the two colored waves. That combined shape is exactly what reaches your ear as a chord.

4 Circles riding on circles

Here's the trick behind this whole tool. Attach a second circle to the edge of the first, and a third to that one. Each spins at its own rate. Follow the very tip of the chain and it traces a surprisingly intricate path.

These stacked circles are called epicycles — the same picture you see on the main Visualizer tab.

5 Fourier's big idea

In the 1800s, Joseph Fourier showed something astonishing: any repeating wave — even a sharp, jagged one — can be built by stacking enough simple sine waves of the right frequencies and sizes.

Watch a flat-topped square wave emerge as we add more and more sine "harmonics." More circles, sharper corners.

6 Back to music

Every instrument adds its own set of harmonics on top of the fundamental note — that's why a flute and a violin playing the same pitch sound different. This tool lets you see and hear all of it.
